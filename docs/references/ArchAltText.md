# Arch-Alt-Text Framework

A systematic approach to describing architectural images for accessibility, developed for blind and low-vision architecture students.

## Core Principle

Architectural images convey spatial information that sighted people absorb instantly. Descriptions must reconstruct this understanding through words, moving from the general to the specific, like peeling layers of an onion.

## The Three Layers

### Layer 1: Macro (The Overview)

**Purpose**: Orient the listener to what they're about to learn about.

**Content** (3 sentences):
1. **Medium**: What type of image is this?
2. **Subject**: What does it show?
3. **Purpose**: Why does it matter?

**Example**:
> This is a floor plan of a two-bedroom apartment. The drawing shows a compact urban unit of approximately 850 square feet. The design demonstrates how a small footprint can accommodate open living while maintaining bedroom privacy.

### Layer 2: Meso (The Composition)

**Purpose**: Establish the overall structure and relationships.

**Content** (4+ sentences):
1. **Organization**: How is it arranged?
2. **Relationships**: How do parts connect?
3. **Orientation**: How is it positioned?
4. **Scale**: How big is it?

**Example**:
> The apartment occupies a roughly rectangular footprint, 25 feet wide by 34 feet deep. Living spaces cluster near the entry on the south side, while bedrooms occupy the quieter north end. A central corridor connects public and private zones, with the bathroom positioned as a buffer between them. Windows appear on the south and west walls, suggesting a corner unit.

### Layer 3: Micro (The Details)

**Purpose**: Provide the specifics needed for deep understanding.

**Content** (8+ sentences):
1. **Entry sequence**: Where and how do you enter?
2. **Spatial progression**: How do you move through?
3. **Specific elements**: What are the key features?
4. **Dimensions**: How big are things?
5. **Materials**: What are things made of? (if visible)
6. **Analogies**: What familiar things compare?
7. **Wayfinding**: How would you navigate?
8. **Notable features**: What stands out?

## Spatial Description Techniques

### Establishing Position

Always clarify the reference point:
- "As you enter through the main door..."
- "Standing in the center of the living room, facing north..."
- "From the kitchen island, looking toward the windows..."

### Describing Movement

Use path-based descriptions:
- "Walking from the entry, you pass the coat closet on your left before the hallway opens into the living area"
- "To reach the bedroom, turn right from the living room and continue past the bathroom door"

### Conveying Scale

Use multiple reference systems:
- **Dimensions**: "The room measures 12 by 15 feet"
- **Familiar objects**: "About the size of a standard two-car garage"
- **Body scale**: "Wide enough for two people to walk side by side"
- **Furniture**: "Space for a queen bed with nightstands, plus a small desk"

### Describing Shapes

Use simple geometric terms with elaboration:
- "The room is L-shaped, with the longer arm running east-west and the shorter arm extending south"
- "The wall curves gently inward, like the inside of a shallow bowl"
- "The ceiling slopes from 12 feet at the window wall down to 9 feet at the back"

## Drawing Type Guidelines

### Floor Plans

Key elements to describe:
- Overall shape and dimensions
- Wall locations (what's solid, what's open)
- Door positions and swing directions
- Window locations
- Room names and sizes
- Circulation paths
- Vertical elements (stairs, elevators)

### Sections

Key elements to describe:
- Ground line and grade
- Floor levels and heights
- Ceiling/roof profiles
- Vertical relationships
- Material indications
- How spaces stack

### Elevations

Key elements to describe:
- Overall building form
- Window and door patterns
- Roof profile
- Ground relationship
- Material indications
- Symmetry or asymmetry

### Site Plans

Key elements to describe:
- Building location on site
- Entry approaches
- Outdoor spaces
- Landscape features
- Neighboring context
- Orientation

### Photographs

Key elements to describe:
- Vantage point (inside/outside, level, direction)
- What's in foreground/background
- Lighting conditions
- Material and texture qualities
- Human activity (if present)
- Atmospheric conditions

## Vocabulary Reference

### Spatial Terms

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Adjacent | Next to, sharing a boundary |
| Contiguous | Touching, connected without break |
| Axial | Organized along a line |
| Radial | Organized around a center point |
| Orthogonal | At right angles |
| Diagonal | At an angle to the main grid |

### Relational Terms

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Opens to | Connects without physical barrier |
| Overlooks | Has visual access to (from above) |
| Accessible from | Can be reached via |
| Separated by | Divided by a barrier |
| Aligned with | On the same axis as |

### Scale Terms

| Term | Approximate Size |
|------|-----------------|
| Intimate | 6-8 feet, close conversation |
| Human scale | 8-12 feet, comfortable single-story |
| Generous | 12-16 feet, feels spacious |
| Monumental | 20+ feet, grand or institutional |

## Common Pitfalls

### Avoid

1. **Left/Right without reference**: "On the left" means nothing without stating from where
2. **Vague sizes**: "Large room" could mean anything
3. **Color alone**: Colors don't convey texture or material
4. **Aesthetic judgment**: "Beautiful design" doesn't help understanding
5. **Assumed knowledge**: Explain architectural terms
6. **Dense paragraphs**: Break into digestible chunks

### Prefer

1. **Cardinal directions**: "On the north wall"
2. **Specific dimensions**: "Approximately 14 by 18 feet"
3. **Material + texture**: "Rough concrete block walls"
4. **Objective description**: "The window spans floor to ceiling"
5. **Defined terms**: "The clerestory—a row of windows near the ceiling—"
6. **Layered structure**: Macro → Meso → Micro

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering a description:

- [ ] Three clear layers (Macro, Meso, Micro)?
- [ ] Reference points established?
- [ ] Dimensions or scale references included?
- [ ] Analogies to familiar things?
- [ ] Wayfinding instructions?
- [ ] Architectural terms explained?
- [ ] No left/right without context?
- [ ] Follow-up options offered?
