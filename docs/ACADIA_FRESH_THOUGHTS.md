## 4. What's Not Working (Diagnosis)

Numbered for ease of reference. Each item cites where in the draft it shows up.

1. **The thesis is buried.** Outline B's central claim — that culture, not tools, is the deeper barrier — barely appears in the body. The introduction opens with "Architecture education traditionally relies heavily on visual representation," which is true but generic. 2. **The Code section over-describes.** The full "Exposed Workflows" list (A–I, with sub-bullets) reads as software documentation, not as evidence for a research argument. An ACADIA reviewer doesn't need the bounding-box reporter, the OSC listener, or the per-part volume report enumerated. Right now there is no framework section in the body — just a prompt in the appendix. This inverts the value.

5. **"Five Sites of Cultural Translation" doesn't exist.** Outline B §4 promises ~1,500 words on five specific sites: lecture slides, studio critiques, precedent study, peer learning, assessment. Each with current state, translation mechanism, and unsolved challenges. None of this is written. This is the paper's spine. Without it, the cultural-translation argument has no working examples.

6. **The legal/DRES/IITAA section does not belong.** This is roughly 800 words of UIUC compliance content. ACADIA reviewers do not need Section 504, Title II, IITAA, or DRES procedural detail. **One sentence with a footnote** covers what a reviewer needs. 7. **SME bios do not belong in the body.** Move to acknowledgments. Cite collaborators by name and role in one sentence each, or in a footnote. The biographical detail (Bob Dignan's 15 years in educational media, Ann's Carthage BA) is institutional document tone, not research paper tone.

8. **Peer learning and assessment treatments are stubs.** The takeaways gesture at both, but neither is theorized. Outline B says assessment is "the largest gap" — that claim needs at least 200 words of treatment to land. Peer learning is even thinner.

9. **The bibliography in the current draft is sparse.** `ACADIA_BRAINSTORM_REARRANGED.md` has already mapped 136 sources organized by relevance. The paper's actual bibliography should be 20–35 of those, but the current draft uses fewer than ten, mostly disconnected from the field's contemporary conversation.

10. **Editor TODOs are still in the body.** "(needs a rewrite)", "(needs an edit)", "Flesh this out", "Not sure this section is relevant to our paper, consider omitting", "**Double check this with Ann's revisions". A reviewer should never see these. Mark them in a separate file or use comments, but get them out of the body before final pass.

11. **Tone shifts.** The voice ranges from research-paper formal to internal-memo casual to TODO-list-imperative within single paragraphs. Pick a voice (we recommend: precise, present-tense, third-person on the project, first-person plural on authorial moves) and enforce it on a final pass.

12. **The student collaborator is barely visible.** The paper's empirical grounding is one student over one academic year. That's the case study. Right now Daniel — or whoever — appears as "the student" in passing. Outline B is implicitly making an empirical claim from a single case; that case needs to be foregrounded, characterized, and *honestly framed as n=1*. Otherwise the limitations section will land as defensive rather than principled.

13. **Title work.** "Alt-Text Is Not Enough" is punchy and earns its keep — it frames the contribution against a known concept. Consider whether the subtitle should explicitly name the contribution. Working candidate: *"Alt-Text Is Not Enough: Translating the Visual Culture of Architecture Education for Blind Participation."* Avoid the words "AI-driven" and "transformative" — both flagged in `del Campo` discourse, both hollow at ACADIA in 2026.

14. **Two co-authors, one voice.** Make sure Hugh and John have agreed on which sections each owns and that the seams aren't visible. The current draft reads in places like a stitched document.

## 9. ACADIA Reviewer Lens

What a reviewer is likely to praise:

- The Macro/Meso/Micro framework as a portable contribution.
- Field positioning against contemporary text-to-geometry work — *if* you deploy it.
- The honesty of n=1 (if framed as principled rather than apologetic).
- The Copper House 2 example as concrete evidence.
- The cultural-translation reframing — this is genuinely a fresh move for ACADIA.

What a reviewer will push back on (and what to pre-empt):

- **"Is this architecture or accessibility?"** Pre-empt by anchoring §2 in architectural pedagogy (Gissen, Heylighen, the visual culture of critique). Make it impossible to read as a generic accessibility paper.
- **"What is the *computational design* contribution?"** Pre-empt by foregrounding M/M/M as a structured, replicable framework — a method, not just a prompt. Cite Lundgard & Satyanarayan to claim a lineage.
- **"n=1 is not generalizable."** Pre-empt by being honest in §1 and §6. Frame as: this paper's contribution is the framework and the diagnosis; the case grounds the claims but does not exhaust them. Echo Heylighen's empirical-study posture explicitly.
- **"You overclaim for AI."** Pre-empt with the trust-but-verify framing already in the draft, and by being precise about what AI does (description of static images) versus what it cannot yet do reliably (real-time critique narration, peer feedback translation).
- **"Where is the system / artifact?"** ACADIA values systems. The CLI is a real artifact; mention it precisely in §3 or §5 as the substrate that makes the framework operable, but do not let it eat the paper.
- **"The legal/policy material is out of place."** Cut it. (Pre-empted.)
